# Understanding Credit Scores

## What Is a Credit Score?

A credit score is a numerical summary of your credit history that lenders use to estimate how likely you are to repay a loan. In the UK, the main credit reference agencies are Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion; each uses a slightly different scale but all draw on similar underlying data.

## What Goes Into Your Score?

| Factor | Typical Weight | What It Measures |
|--------|---------------|------------------|
| Payment history | ~35% | Whether you pay on time |
| Credit utilisation | ~30% | How much of your available credit you use |
| Length of credit history | ~15% | How long your accounts have been open |
| Credit mix | ~10% | Variety of credit types (card, loan, mortgage) |
| New credit enquiries | ~10% | Recent applications for new credit |

## Credit Utilisation

Utilisation is your current balance divided by your credit limit, expressed as a percentage. Keeping utilisation below 30% is generally advised; below 10% is better still. If you have a £5,000 credit limit and carry a £1,500 balance, your utilisation is 30%.

## How to Improve Your Score

### Quick Wins (1–3 Months)
- Pay every bill and debt payment on time — even one missed payment can significantly lower your score.
- Pay down balances to reduce utilisation.
- Correct any errors on your credit report (all three agencies must provide a free statutory report on request).

### Medium-Term Improvements (6–12 Months)
- Keep old accounts open — closing them shortens your average credit history.
- Avoid applying for multiple credit products in a short period; each hard enquiry temporarily lowers your score.
- Register on the electoral roll at your current address.

### Longer-Term Factors (1+ Years)
- Maintain a consistent payment record over time.
- Gradually increase credit limits (without increasing spending) to lower utilisation.

## Hard vs. Soft Enquiries

A **hard enquiry** occurs when a lender checks your credit in response to a credit application; it temporarily reduces your score by a few points and remains visible for up to two years. A **soft enquiry** (such as checking your own score or a pre-approval check) has no effect on your score.

## Common Myths

- **Checking your own score hurts it**: False — checking your own score is a soft enquiry with no impact.
- **Closing old accounts always helps**: False — closing accounts reduces available credit and shortens history, often lowering the score.
- **Carrying a small balance is better than paying in full**: False — paying in full every month is optimal for both your score and your finances.
