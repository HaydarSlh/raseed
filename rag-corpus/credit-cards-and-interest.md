# Credit Cards and How Interest Works

## How Credit Card Interest Works

Your interest rate, expressed as an **Annual Percentage Rate (APR)**, is what you pay
for borrowing money on the card. Most issuers calculate interest **daily**, applying
a daily periodic rate to your outstanding balance. The sooner you pay down what you
owe, the less interest accumulates.

## Fixed vs. Variable APR

Credit card interest rates are either **fixed** or **variable**:

- A **fixed** rate stays the same unless the issuer notifies you of a change.
- A **variable** rate is tied to an interest rate index and rises or falls as that
  index moves.

When comparing cards, check whether the APR is fixed or variable so you understand
how your rate may change over time.

## The Grace Period: How to Avoid Interest

Most cards offer a **grace period** — if you pay your full statement balance by the
due date, you are not charged interest on new purchases. If you carry a balance,
you typically lose the grace period, and interest may be charged from the date of
each purchase until you pay in full again.

**The simplest way to avoid interest is to pay your statement balance in full every
month.**

## Choosing a Card

Match the card to how you will use it. If you plan to carry a balance, a lower APR
matters most. If you pay in full each month, rewards and fees may matter more than
the APR. Always compare fees, not just the headline rate.

## Fees and Your Protections

- Issuers must give you **45 days' advance notice** before making significant changes
  to your account terms, such as raising your rate.
- If you report a card lost or stolen **before** it is used, you cannot be held
  responsible for unauthorized charges. Even if it is used, federal law caps your
  liability for unauthorized credit card charges at **$50**.
- Watch for "mystery" fees on your statement and question any charge you don't
  recognize.

## Paying Down Credit Card Debt

- Always pay at least the minimum on time to avoid late fees and credit damage, but
  pay more than the minimum whenever you can — paying only the minimum stretches the
  debt out for years and multiplies the interest you pay.
- Two common strategies: pay off the **highest-interest** balance first (saves the
  most money), or pay off the **smallest** balance first (builds momentum).
- Avoid adding new charges to a card while you are trying to pay it down.
