# Budgeting Basics

## What Is a Budget?

A budget is a plan for how you will allocate your income across expenses, savings, and financial goals over a given period — typically a month. A budget does not restrict spending arbitrarily; it gives every pound a purpose so you know in advance where your money will go.

## Why Budget?

Without a plan, spending often expands to fill available income. A budget reveals where money actually goes (often a surprise), flags categories that exceed limits, and creates room for savings and goals that would otherwise be crowded out.

## Step 1: Know Your Income

Start with your **net income** — what lands in your account after tax and deductions. Include all reliable sources: salary, freelance income, benefits, rental income. Use the lower figure if your income varies.

## Step 2: List Your Expenses

Separate expenses into two types:

- **Fixed expenses**: the same amount every period — rent, mortgage, loan payments, insurance premiums, subscriptions.
- **Variable expenses**: change month to month — groceries, utilities, transport, dining, entertainment, clothing.

Use three months of bank or card statements to calculate realistic averages for variable categories.

## Step 3: Choose a Framework

### The 50/30/20 Rule

Allocate roughly:
- **50%** of net income to needs (housing, food, utilities, transport, minimum debt payments)
- **30%** to wants (dining out, streaming, hobbies, holidays)
- **20%** to savings and debt repayment above minimums

This is a starting point, not a strict rule. High-cost cities may require 60% for needs; high debt loads may demand more than 20% for repayment.

### Zero-Based Budgeting

Assign every pound of income to a category until income minus all allocations equals zero. This maximises intentionality but requires more tracking.

### Envelope Method

Withdraw cash for variable categories and put each amount in a physical or digital envelope. When the envelope is empty, spending in that category stops for the month.

## Step 4: Track and Adjust

A budget is only useful if you compare actual spending against planned amounts. Review weekly or at month-end, identify overruns, and adjust either spending or the category limit.

## Common Budgeting Mistakes

- **Forgetting irregular expenses**: annual insurance, car servicing, school supplies. Divide by 12 and include a monthly sinking-fund contribution.
- **Setting unrealistic limits**: a budget you cannot follow will be abandoned. Start with slight reductions rather than dramatic cuts.
- **Not budgeting for fun**: a budget with zero entertainment or dining will fail. Build in discretionary spending; guilt-free money helps adherence.

## Irregular Income

If your income varies, budget based on your lowest expected monthly income. In better months, direct the surplus to savings or debt repayment, not lifestyle inflation.
