# Investing Basics

## Why Invest?

Savings accounts protect purchasing power over the short term but rarely beat inflation over the long term. Investing in assets that grow — stocks, bonds, property — historically produces real returns above inflation, allowing wealth to compound over time.

Investing carries risk: values can fall as well as rise. The key principle is matching investment risk to your time horizon and risk tolerance.

## Core Concepts

### Risk and Return

Higher potential returns almost always come with higher risk. A diversified portfolio of stocks historically returns 7–10% per year on average, but individual years can range from –40% to +50%. Bonds and cash are lower return but more stable.

### Compounding

Compounding means your returns themselves generate returns. £10,000 invested for 30 years at 7% per year grows to approximately £76,000 — without adding another penny. Starting early is the single most powerful factor in long-term wealth building.

### Diversification

Spreading investments across asset classes (stocks, bonds, property), geographies, and sectors reduces the risk that any single bad outcome destroys the portfolio. A diversified fund holds hundreds or thousands of assets simultaneously.

## Main Asset Classes

| Asset Class | Expected Return | Risk Level | Best Time Horizon |
|-------------|----------------|------------|-------------------|
| Cash / savings | 2–5% | Very low | Short-term (0–2 years) |
| Government bonds | 3–6% | Low–medium | Short to medium (2–7 years) |
| Corporate bonds | 4–8% | Medium | Medium (3–7 years) |
| Global equity (stocks) | 7–10% | High | Long-term (7+ years) |
| Property | 5–9% | Medium–high | Long-term (10+ years) |

## Common Investment Vehicles

### Index Funds and ETFs

Index funds passively track a market index (e.g., the FTSE All-World). They offer broad diversification at very low cost. Most evidence shows that low-cost index funds outperform the majority of actively managed funds over the long run, primarily because of lower fees.

### Stocks and Shares ISA (UK)

A Stocks and Shares ISA allows you to invest up to £20,000 per tax year; all returns — dividends and capital gains — are tax-free inside the wrapper. This is typically the first investment vehicle for most UK residents after workplace pensions.

### Workplace Pension

Employer pension contributions are essentially free money. Contributing enough to capture the full employer match is typically the highest-return financial decision available — a 50% or 100% immediate return on your contribution before any market movement.

## Getting Started

1. Ensure you have an emergency fund before investing — you should not need to sell investments at an inopportune time.
2. Clear high-interest debt first (credit cards, personal loans above ~7%).
3. Maximise employer pension match.
4. Open a Stocks and Shares ISA for medium-to-long-term goals.
5. Choose a low-cost global index fund.
6. Invest consistently (monthly) regardless of market conditions — this is called pound-cost averaging and smooths out market timing risk.

## Common Mistakes

- **Waiting for the "right time"**: time in the market beats timing the market for long-term investors.
- **Panic-selling during downturns**: temporary falls are normal; selling locks in losses and misses the recovery.
- **Ignoring fees**: a 1% annual charge vs. 0.1% compounds into tens of thousands of pounds of difference over 30 years.
- **Concentrating in one stock or sector**: reduces diversification and amplifies risk without proportionate return benefit.
