# Credit Reports and Credit Scores

## What Is a Credit Report?

A credit report is a compilation of information about your financial history,
maintained by credit reporting companies (also called credit bureaus). It records
your credit accounts, payment history, balances, and how long you have been using
credit. The three nationwide credit reporting companies are Equifax, Experian, and
TransUnion. Lenders, landlords, and others use this information to decide whether to
do business with you and on what terms.

## What Is a Credit Score?

Your credit report and your credit score play different roles. A credit score is a
number, calculated from the information in your credit report, that predicts how
likely you are to repay a loan and make payments on time. Because the score is
derived from the report, the accuracy of your report directly affects your score.

Common factors that affect most credit scores include:

- **Payment history** — whether you pay your bills on time.
- **Amounts owed / credit utilization** — how much of your available credit you are
  using.
- **Length of credit history** — how long your accounts have been open.
- **New credit** — how many new accounts or applications you have recently.
- **Credit mix** — the variety of credit types you manage.

## Getting Your Credit Report for Free

You have the right to a free copy of your credit report from each of the nationwide
credit reporting companies. The CFPB recommends reviewing your reports at least once
a year to make sure there are no errors that could keep you from getting credit, or
from getting the best available terms on a loan.

**Requesting your own credit report does not hurt your credit score.**

## Disputing Errors on Your Report

If you find inaccurate or incomplete information, you have the right to dispute it.
The CFPB recommends submitting your dispute to **both**:

1. The credit reporting company that issued the report, and
2. The company that provided the disputed information (the "furnisher," such as your
   lender or card issuer).

If you cannot resolve the issue directly, you can submit a complaint to the CFPB,
which will forward it to the company and work to get you a response, generally
within 15 days.

## Building and Keeping a Good Score

- Pay all your bills on time, every time.
- Keep your credit card balances low relative to your limits.
- Avoid opening many new accounts in a short period.
- Keep older accounts open to lengthen your credit history.
- Check your reports regularly and correct errors promptly.
