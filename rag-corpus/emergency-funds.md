# Emergency Funds

## What Is an Emergency Fund?

An emergency fund is a dedicated pool of savings set aside to cover unexpected expenses or income disruptions. It is kept separate from your regular savings and investments so you can access it immediately without selling assets or incurring debt.

## How Much Should You Save?

### The Standard Guideline

Most financial guidance recommends keeping **three to six months of essential living expenses** in your emergency fund. Essential expenses include rent or mortgage, utilities, groceries, insurance premiums, and minimum debt payments.

### Factors That Affect the Right Amount

- **Job security**: Employees in stable, in-demand fields can lean toward three months; freelancers or those in volatile industries should aim for six months or more.
- **Number of income earners**: A dual-income household has a natural buffer; a single-income household needs a larger cushion.
- **Dependants**: Children, elderly relatives, or others who rely on you increase the recommended target.
- **Health considerations**: Chronic conditions or high-deductible health plans justify a larger fund.

## Where to Keep It

An emergency fund must be liquid and stable in value. Suitable accounts include:

- **High-yield savings accounts** — FDIC-insured (or equivalent), earns modest interest, accessible within one to two business days.
- **Money-market accounts** — similar safety and liquidity, sometimes slightly higher yield.

Avoid stocks, bonds, or investment accounts for your emergency fund; market downturns may coincide exactly with when you need the money.

## Building Your Emergency Fund

### Starting from Zero

1. Open a dedicated savings account separate from your everyday spending.
2. Set a small, achievable initial goal (e.g., one month of expenses).
3. Automate a fixed transfer on every payday, even if it is small.
4. Direct windfalls — tax refunds, bonuses, gifts — to the fund until the target is reached.

### When You Have Some Savings Already

Calculate your monthly essential expenses, multiply by your target months, and subtract what you already have. The gap is what you need to save.

## Using Your Emergency Fund

An emergency fund is for genuine emergencies: job loss, a medical crisis, a major car repair, or a boiler replacement. It is **not** for holidays, discretionary upgrades, or expected costs (like annual insurance renewals) that you can plan for in your regular budget.

After using the fund, make rebuilding it the next financial priority before resuming investing or other goals.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating it as a slush fund**: Using the emergency fund for non-emergencies erodes its protective purpose.
- **Keeping it in a checking account**: Mixing it with everyday money makes it easy to spend accidentally; a separate account adds friction.
- **Never revisiting the target**: Your expenses change over time; recalculate every year or after major life changes.
