# APR vs APY Explained

## Overview

Two rates appear on almost every financial product: **APR** (Annual Percentage Rate) and **APY** (Annual Percentage Yield). They look similar but measure different things. Understanding the difference helps you compare loans, credit cards, and savings accounts accurately.

## APR — Annual Percentage Rate

APR is the yearly cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage. It includes the interest rate plus most mandatory fees, but it does **not** account for compounding within the year.

### Where APR Appears

- Credit cards
- Personal loans
- Mortgages and home-equity loans
- Car loans

### How to Use APR

A lower APR on a loan means lower borrowing cost — but only when comparing products with the same term and fee structure. Some lenders advertise a low APR while burying high origination fees; always confirm which fees are included.

## APY — Annual Percentage Yield

APY reflects the true annual return on a deposit after compounding is applied. Because interest earned in earlier periods itself earns interest in later periods, APY is always equal to or greater than the nominal interest rate.

### The Compounding Effect

If a savings account pays 5% nominal interest compounded monthly, the APY is approximately 5.12%. The more frequently interest compounds (daily vs. monthly vs. annually), the higher the APY relative to the nominal rate.

### Where APY Appears

- Savings accounts
- Certificates of deposit (CDs)
- Money-market accounts
- High-yield savings accounts

## Comparing APR and APY

| Situation | Which Rate Matters |
|-----------|--------------------|
| Taking out a loan or using a credit card | APR — lower is better |
| Opening a savings or deposit account | APY — higher is better |

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Credit Card

A card with 20% APR charges roughly 1.67% per month on the outstanding balance. If you carry a £1,000 balance for one year, the annual interest cost is close to £200 — but if compounding applies (as it does with most credit cards), the effective cost is higher.

### Example 2: Savings Account

Account A offers 4.8% APY; Account B offers 4.8% nominal rate compounded quarterly. Account A's effective yield is higher because daily compounding on a 4.8% nominal rate produces an APY above 4.9%. Always compare APY when choosing where to save.

## Key Takeaways

- **Borrowing**: compare APR — it captures the full cost of a loan in a single number.
- **Saving**: compare APY — it reflects your actual annual earnings after compounding.
- Never compare an APR on one product with an APY on another; they measure different things.
