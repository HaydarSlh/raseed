# Avoiding Fraud and Scams

## Common Types of Scams

The CFPB highlights several common fraud schemes:

- **Identity theft** — someone steals your personal information to commit fraud.
- **Imposter scams** — a fraudster pretends to be someone you trust, such as a
  government agency, your bank, a relative, or tech support.
- **Phishing** — deceptive emails, texts, or calls designed to trick you into
  revealing passwords, account numbers, or other sensitive information.
- **Wire and money-transfer fraud** — pressure to send money in ways that are hard to
  reverse.
- **Foreclosure relief scams** — targeting homeowners in financial distress.
- **Elder financial exploitation** — targeting older adults and other vulnerable
  people.
- **Mail fraud** — fraudulent schemes carried out through the postal service.

## Warning Signs

Be on alert when you notice:

- **Pressure to act immediately** — scammers create urgency so you don't stop to
  think.
- **Unexpected requests** for personal information, passwords, or payment.
- **Unusual payment methods** — requests to pay with gift cards, wire transfers, or
  cryptocurrency are a strong red flag, because these are difficult to trace or
  recover.
- Contact you did not initiate, claiming to be from a trusted organization.

A legitimate organization will not pressure you to pay on the spot or demand payment
by gift card.

## How to Protect Yourself

- Add your number to the **National Do Not Call Registry**.
- Place a **fraud alert** or a **security freeze** on your credit reports with the
  credit bureaus.
- Monitor your accounts and statements regularly for transactions you don't
  recognize.
- Never share passwords, PINs, or one-time codes, and verify requests by contacting
  the organization through a number you look up yourself.

## If You've Been Scammed

1. **Contact your financial institution immediately** — the sooner you report
   unauthorized activity, the better your chances of recovering funds.
2. **Report the fraud to the Federal Trade Commission** at **reportfraud.ftc.gov**.
3. For suspected elder exploitation, contact your local **Adult Protective
   Services**.
4. You can reach the **CFPB at (855) 411-2372** for help and to submit a complaint.

Acting quickly is the single most important step — many protections depend on
reporting unauthorized transactions promptly.
