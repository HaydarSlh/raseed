# Building an Emergency Fund

## Why You Need One

An emergency fund is money set aside to cover unexpected expenses or a drop in
income. Without it, a financial shock — a car repair, a medical bill, or a loss of
work — can have lasting consequences. People who lack savings often turn to credit
cards or loans, creating debt that is harder to pay off, or they drain retirement
savings. The CFPB notes that individuals who struggle to recover from a financial
shock have less savings to protect against the next one.

## How Much to Save

There is no single right answer. Instead of a fixed rule, reflect on the unexpected
expenses you have faced in the past to set a target that fits your situation. Even a
small amount can provide meaningful security, especially if you are living
paycheck to paycheck. Starting small and saving consistently matters more than
hitting a large number immediately.

## Strategies to Build Your Fund

**Create a savings habit.** Set a specific goal, put a system in place to contribute
regularly, track your progress, and celebrate when you reach a milestone.

**Manage your cash flow.** Track when money comes in versus when bills are due. If
the timing is tight, you can ask creditors to adjust your due dates to line up
better with your income.

**Put windfalls to work.** Tax refunds, gifts, and work bonuses are chances to boost
your savings quickly without changing your day-to-day spending.

**Automate your saving.** Set up an automatic recurring transfer to savings, or split
your direct deposit so part of each paycheck goes straight into a separate account.
Removing the decision each month makes saving easier to stick with.

**Save through your employer.** Many employers let you divide your paycheck across
multiple accounts via direct deposit.

## Where to Keep It

Your emergency fund should be safe and easy to reach. Good options include a
dedicated savings account at a bank or credit union, or a prepaid card. Keeping the
money separate from your everyday checking account reduces the temptation to spend
it. Holding it as physical cash is risky because it can be lost or stolen.

## Using and Rebuilding the Fund

Do not hesitate to use the fund when you face a genuine emergency — that is what it
is for. Once you have drawn it down, make rebuilding it a priority, using the same
strategies that worked when you first built it.
