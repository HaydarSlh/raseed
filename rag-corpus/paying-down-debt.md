# Paying Down Debt

## Understanding Your Debt

Before choosing a repayment strategy, list every debt you carry:

| Debt | Outstanding Balance | Interest Rate (APR) | Minimum Payment |
|------|--------------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Credit card A | £2,400 | 22% | £48 |
| Personal loan | £8,000 | 11% | £180 |
| Student loan | £15,000 | 5% | £80 |

This snapshot tells you the total burden and the interest cost of each debt — essential for choosing a strategy.

## The Two Main Strategies

### Avalanche Method (Mathematically Optimal)

Pay the minimum on every debt, then direct all extra money to the debt with the **highest interest rate**. Once that is cleared, roll its payment into the next-highest-rate debt.

**Advantage**: pays the least total interest over time.  
**Challenge**: the highest-rate debt may not be the smallest balance, so visible progress can feel slow.

### Snowball Method (Psychologically Effective)

Pay the minimum on every debt, then direct extra money to the debt with the **smallest balance**. Once that is cleared, roll its payment to the next smallest.

**Advantage**: quick wins — clearing a debt entirely provides motivation to continue.  
**Challenge**: may cost more in total interest than the avalanche method.

**Which to choose**: if the interest-rate difference between your debts is small (e.g., within 2–3 percentage points), the snowball's motivational benefits often outweigh the small extra cost. If one debt carries a rate far above others (e.g., 25% credit card vs. 6% car loan), the avalanche saves significantly more.

## Increasing Your Repayment Rate

- **Find room in the budget**: review variable expenses for categories to cut temporarily.
- **Apply windfalls**: direct tax refunds, bonuses, and gifts to debt before lifestyle spending.
- **Negotiate lower rates**: call your card issuer and ask for a rate reduction; long-standing customers with a good payment history often succeed.
- **Balance transfers**: moving high-rate credit-card debt to a 0% promotional card can freeze interest for 12–24 months — but watch for transfer fees and ensure you can clear the balance before the promotional period ends.
- **Debt consolidation loans**: combining multiple debts at a lower rate simplifies payments and reduces cost, but only if you don't continue using the cards that were paid off.

## The Debt–Savings Balance

While carrying high-interest debt (above ~6–7%), prioritise repayment over investing — the guaranteed return of eliminating a 20% APR card typically outweighs expected investment gains. Keep a small emergency fund (one month of expenses minimum) even while aggressively repaying, to avoid taking on new debt when something unexpected arises.

## Common Mistakes

- **Making only minimum payments**: on a £2,000 credit card at 20% APR, paying only the minimum can take over a decade to clear and cost more in interest than the original balance.
- **Taking on new debt while repaying**: stops progress and demoralises.
- **Treating debt repayment as a permanent state**: once high-rate debt is cleared, redirect those payments to savings and investment.
