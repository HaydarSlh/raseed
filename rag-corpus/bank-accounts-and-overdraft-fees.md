# Bank Accounts and Overdraft Fees

## Choosing an Account

Bank and credit union accounts generally fall into two main types: **checking
accounts** for everyday spending and bill payment, and **savings accounts** for
setting money aside. When opening an account, use a checklist to compare fees,
minimum balance requirements, and features. You do not always need a Social Security
number to open an account, and if you have been denied an account based on past
banking history, you still have options.

## Overdraft and NSF Fees

You can be charged fees when you spend more than the money available in your account:

- **Overdraft fee.** Charged when the bank covers a transaction that takes your
  balance below zero. This can happen with checks, electronic payments, or debit
  card transactions.
- **Non-sufficient funds (NSF) fee.** Charged when the bank declines or returns a
  transaction because you don't have enough money.

### You Choose Whether to Opt In

For ATM withdrawals and one-time debit card purchases, banks generally **cannot**
charge you overdraft fees unless you have **opted in** to overdraft coverage. If you
do not opt in, those transactions are simply declined when you lack the funds — and
you avoid the fee. Review whether you are opted in, and opt out if you would rather
have transactions declined than pay overdraft fees.

### How to Reduce Fees

- Keep a small cushion in your account.
- Sign up for low-balance alerts.
- Link a savings account for overdraft transfers (usually cheaper than an overdraft
  fee).
- Decline overdraft coverage on debit and ATM transactions.

## When Deposited Funds Become Available

Federal law sets maximum hold times for deposited checks, but each bank or credit
union has its own rules about when you can access the money. Be aware of your bank's
funds-availability policy so you don't spend against a check that hasn't cleared.

## Your Protections

- **Federal benefits** such as Social Security and VA payments are generally
  protected from garnishment by debt collectors.
- You have recourse for **unauthorized transactions** and missing funds — report them
  to your bank promptly.
- You can stop automatic payments, use online bill pay, and transfer your account to
  another institution. If a bank doesn't resolve a problem, you can submit a
  complaint to the CFPB.
