# Dealing With Debt Collectors

## Your Rights Under Federal Law

If a debt collector contacts you, you have protections under the **Fair Debt
Collection Practices Act (FDCPA)**. Debt collectors are not allowed to use unfair,
deceptive, or abusive practices. The CFPB's **Debt Collection Rule** (effective
November 30, 2021) clarifies how collectors can communicate with you and what
information they are required to provide.

## The Validation Notice

When a collector first contacts you, they generally must send a **validation notice**
describing the debt — the amount, the creditor, and your rights. If you dispute the
debt in writing within 30 days of receiving the notice, the collector must pause
collection until they verify the debt. Always make sure a debt is really yours and
the amount is correct before paying.

## What Collectors Can and Cannot Do

- **Limits on contact frequency.** Under the Debt Collection Rule, a collector who
  contacts you about a particular debt more than seven times within seven days is
  presumed to be harassing you.
- **Right to make them stop contacting you.** You can tell a collector, in writing,
  to stop contacting you. After that, they may only confirm they will stop or tell
  you about a specific action, such as a lawsuit.
- **No harassment or false statements.** Collectors cannot threaten you, use obscene
  language, lie about the amount you owe, or falsely claim to be attorneys or
  government representatives.
- **Protected benefits.** Only in rare cases can debt collectors take Social Security
  or Veterans Affairs (VA) benefits.

## When You Don't Recognize the Debt

If you believe you do not owe the debt, or you want more information, ask the
collector — in writing — to verify it. Do not assume a debt is valid just because a
collector says so. Scammers sometimes try to collect on debts that don't exist or
that you have already paid.

## Statute of Limitations (Time-Barred Debt)

Every debt has a **statute of limitations** — a time limit, set by state law, after
which a collector can no longer sue you to collect. A debt past this limit is called
"time-barred." Be careful: making a payment or even acknowledging an old debt can
sometimes restart the clock. If you are unsure, find out your state's limit before
you pay or promise to pay.

## Responding to a Collector

Don't ignore a debt collector — especially if you are sued, because failing to
respond can lead to a default judgment against you. Know what the debt is, confirm it
is yours, understand your rights, and respond promptly. If a collector breaks the
law, you can submit a complaint to the CFPB.
